## Links

- Link full titles or surface names.
- Avoid multiple hyperlinked phrases in a single sentence. Consider bullets if you're linking to multiple destinations within the same sentence.
- Avoid generic terms like "click here".
- Do not list out URLs in full, use unique text.

For specific guidance on how to hyperlink within different surfaces and content types, please refer to the [featured templates](https://blockcell.sqprod.co/sites/writers-block/docs/support/content-type-templates).

## Instruction lists and steps

Our approach to writing instruction lists and steps builds on Block-level guidance for lists. This guidance shares when and how to use instruction lists or steps in support content:

### When to use

- Describing a workflow, troubleshooting guidance, and/or instructions
- Information is sequential. This means you can't change the order of the items without losing their meaning

### Tone

Technical, precise, instructional, and clear.

### Structure

- Use plain, direct language
- Avoid jargon
- Use present tense when possible
- Use sentence punctuation, no exclamation marks
- For clarity, aim for one level of hierarchy, or a maximum of two
- Two levels of hierarchy are generally used when instructions can be completed on more than one surface
- Some exceptions can be made for internal steps. However, if hierarchy exceeds three levels, consider creating a new article

### Format

- Use ordinal numbers
- Use one action per step
- Match system and UI references
- Use exact names of buttons, menu items, fields, system messages
- **Bold UI text** that users will interact with
- Begin with verbs
- Be as specific as possible without getting too technical

#### ✅ Do

- Close

#### ❌ Don't

- Terminate

## Apologies

We respect customers' time and frustrations, especially when their issues affect their business or finances. To achieve this, we practice empathy, not sympathy. Please note that this apology guidance refers to how we write published support content that is shared with customers (like macros), and does not include all support team member interactions or training.

- Do not use apology language unless a genuine mistake occurred—and never in published content
- Focus on solutions and encouragement rather than sympathy
- Preserve apologies for key moments where we screwed up, such as a response to acknowledge longer-than-usual wait times for support or a process failing
- Avoid using "we apologize for the inconvenience." Almost nothing about a customer's problem is just an inconvenience

## Support team terminology and usage guidelines

Write about support from the customer's perspective rather than emphasizing our role in providing it. Use the term "support" in lowercase when talking about the help or assistance customers will receive, as this:

- Keeps the focus on the customer's needs rather than us as an organization
- Hones in on support as a tool for helping customers succeed, not about promoting our services

#### ✅ Do

- Get support
- Contact support

#### ❌ Don't

- Get Square support
- Get Cash App support

When referring to interactions with the business-specific support team, use "[business name] Support" to clearly establish that customers are interacting with authentic team members.

If needed, describe the specific tier of support that customers have access to (like priority support) or if a case has been escalated.

#### ✅ Do

- Square Support
- Someone from Square Support will contact you.
- Access to priority support

#### ❌ Don't

- Square support
- Someone from Support will contact you.
- Access to Priority Support

### External-facing content

When referring to our support team, we use the following terms consistently across external-facing content:

- Each term has a specific tone, clarity level, and ideal use case
- Select based on the context, emotional intensity, and need for clarity
- Use sentence case for each instance

#### A support team member

- **Formality:** Medium
- **Tone:** Neutral
- **Clarity level:** ✅ High
- **Use cases:** Help Center articles, onboarding instructions, chatbot system messages
- **Why and when to use:** Use this as your default term for structured content where users need clear, role-specific language. It strikes a balance between being professional and approachable while emphasizing team affiliation.

#### Someone from our support team

- **Formality:** Medium
- **Tone:** Personal
- **Clarity level:** ✅ High
- **Use cases:** Escalation messages, handoffs, email/chat conversations
- **Why and when to use:** Ideal for human-centered interactions, especially during handoffs or delays. Use this in real-time conversations or when warmth and reassurance are needed.

#### Our team

- **Formality:** Low
- **Tone:** Warm, casual
- **Clarity level:** ⚠️ Medium
- **Use cases:** Customer emails, apology messages, branded content
- **Why and when to use:** Conversational and brand-forward. Best in informal communication when it's already obvious that the customer is talking to support. Avoid in moments where clarity or precision is essential.

### General usage rules

**Prioritize clarity when describing roles.**  
In instructional, help, or UI content, always choose terms like "Support team member" to make the helper's role unambiguous.

**Use warmer, more personal terms in conversations.**  
In email, chat, or escalation contexts, favor phrases like "someone from our support team" to convey empathy and humanity.

**Avoid vague references when team context isn't obvious.**  
Terms like "our team" or "a team member" should only be used when it's crystal clear to the customer that they're speaking with support.

**Retire internal-only or legacy job titles.**  
Avoid terms like specialist or advocate in public-facing content unless legally necessary. These tend to feel clinical, unclear, or transactional.

### Internal-facing content

Consistent terminology within internal-facing content is essential for clarity and comprehension for support team members across regions and roles. Use the following terms to reflect the correct audience and business-unit context.

| Term | Definition and when to use |
|------|---------------------------|
| **Advocate** | Preferred term for frontline support team members. Use in knowledge articles and team-facing documentation. |
| **Team member** | Use when referencing permissions or roles in Square Dashboard (e.g., "Team member with inventory permissions"). |
| **Support team** | Use when referring broadly to the Block Customer Operations organization. |
| **Customer** | External app or product user. Use instead of "user" in external-facing content. |
| **Seller** | Use in Square-facing internal content to refer to business users of Square products. |

When other teams need to be referenced in internal materials, spell out the full team name on first reference and then refer to the team by its established acronym (where applicable). Use title case when referring to the specific team name. Do not abbreviate team names or substitute team names for general terms.

- Transfer to the Cash Escalation Support (CES) team
- Contact the Policy Governance team

#### ❌ Don't

- Transfer to CES
